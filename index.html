Abstract

Text-to-image (T2I) generative models have achieved strong visual fidelity, but their deployment raises urgent questions around demographic fairness and cultural representation. This paper introduces T2I-BiasBench, a reproducible thirteen-metric framework that quantifies bias across demographic and contextual dimensions in small-scale diffusion models.

We evaluate Stable Diffusion v1.5, BK-SDM Base, and Koala Lightning, with Gemini 2.5 Flash (RLHF-trained) as a high-parameter baseline, over 1,574 generated images spanning Beauty, Doctor, Culture, Animal, and Nature prompts. The framework integrates seven original bias metrics and six newly proposed measures to jointly evaluate fairness, stereotype strength, diversity, faithfulness, and prompt alignment. Results show stereotype amplification in beauty prompts, capability masking effects in medical prompts, and persistent cultural collapse to a narrow festival subset across all models.

Framework

Evaluation Metrics

T2I-BiasBench uses a broad metric panel to capture multiple failure modes instead of overfitting to one bias score.

  • Representation Parity
  • Parity Difference
  • Bias Amplification
  • Shannon Entropy
  • KL Divergence
  • Festival CAS (Contextual Association Score)
  • Composite Bias Score
  • Cultural Accuracy Ratio
  • GMR (Grounded Missing Rate)
  • IEMR (Implicit Element Missing Rate)
  • Hallucination Score
  • Vendi Score
  • CLIP Proxy Score (prompt-caption alignment)

Key Contributions

  1. A thirteen-metric evaluation framework integrating seven original bias metrics with six newly proposed metrics for faithfulness, diversity, and cultural accuracy.
  2. Evidence that SD v1.5 and BK-SDM show Bias Amplification above 1.0 in beauty prompts, indicating stereotype reinforcement beyond training distributions.
  3. Observation that surgical PPE can suppress visible gender cues in doctor prompts, materially affecting measured stereotype behavior.
  4. Quantification of a cultural representation failure where all evaluated models frequently collapse Indian festival prompts into a narrow Holi/Diwali subset.
  5. Evidence that model scale alone does not monotonically predict fairness behavior across prompt families.
